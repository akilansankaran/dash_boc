Executive Summary

Canada operates one of the world's most explicitly economic-selection-oriented immigration systems. Through the Express Entry management system and expanding Provincial Nominee Programs, Canada admitted 405,000 permanent residents in 2024, maintaining one of the highest per-capita immigration rates among OECD countries. The foreign-born share of the population (23.0%) is the highest among G7 nations.

This country report provides disaggregated analysis of admission flows by stream, Express Entry CRS score dynamics, provincial settlement patterns, earnings convergence trajectories, and the demographic profile of Canada's immigrant population. Navigate through the indicator tabs above to explore each dimension.

Key Takeaways

Approximately 58% of permanent residents are admitted through economic programs (Federal Skilled Worker, Canadian Experience Class, Provincial Nominees). This human-capital focus produces an immigrant population with higher average educational attainment than the Canadian-born population, though credential recognition remains an integration barrier.
Since its launch in 2015, Express Entry has achieved a median processing time of approximately 5–6 months for the majority of applicants, compared to multi-year timelines under the predecessor system. Category-based selection rounds introduced in 2023 allow targeted draws for priority occupations.
Provincial Nominee Program nominations have grown from under 1,000 in 1999 to over 100,000 annually, directing a growing share of immigrants to Atlantic Canada, the Prairies, and other regions outside the Toronto-Vancouver-Montreal corridor. Retention rates vary by province.
Despite high initial credentials, immigrants to Canada experience a well-documented initial earnings gap relative to Canadian-born workers. IMDB data show that convergence takes approximately 10–15 years for economic-class immigrants and longer for family-class and refugee arrivals. Credential recognition and Canadian experience requirements are key friction points.

Flow Analysis

Canada's permanent immigration volumes have increased substantially from approximately 85,000 in 1985 to 405,000 in 2024, with the most aggressive growth occurring under the 2021–2023 Immigration Levels Plans. The government's multi-year planning approach - unique among major immigration countries - allows for announced targets that signal anticipated volumes to provinces, settlement agencies, and labor markets.

Source-country composition has shifted dramatically. In 1980, the top five source countries were the United Kingdom, India, Vietnam, Hong Kong, and the Philippines. By 2024, India alone accounts for approximately 25% of all permanent residents, followed by the Philippines, China, Nigeria, and Afghanistan.

Express Entry Analysis

The Express Entry system has undergone significant evolution since its 2015 launch. Initial draws invited candidates with CRS scores above 450; subsequent rounds saw cutoffs decline to the 440–470 range as pool size grew. The introduction of category-based selection rounds in 2023 - targeting healthcare workers, STEM professionals, French-language speakers, trade workers, and agriculture/agri-food workers - has diversified the profile of invited applicants and created multiple effective cutoff tiers within the system.

Processing time has been a key performance indicator. After deterioration during the COVID-19 period (median times exceeding 12 months), IRCC has restored processing to approximately 5–6 months for the majority of Express Entry applications, meeting its stated service standard.

Economic Integration and Earnings

Labour force outcomes, income distributions, poverty measures, and regression-based analyses of economic integration across Canada's provinces and territories. Source: Statistics Canada, Census 2021.

Labour Force & Income Distribution

G7 · Census 2021

Labour Force Indicators by Province/Territory

Provincial participation, employment, and unemployment rates exhibit substantial geographic heterogeneity; YT and NT record the highest participation rates (≈71%) driven by resource-extraction economies and low dependency ratios, while Nunavut's anomalously elevated unemployment rate (≈17%) despite moderate participation is consistent with structural skill-mismatch in remote labour markets. Source: Statistics Canada, Census 2021.

G8 · Census 2021

Income Distribution: Pre-tax vs. After-tax (Canada, 2021)

The after-tax distribution is right-shifted relative to pre-tax income at the $20,000–$40,000 range - reflecting the net effect of refundable credits and transfers - while the pre-tax distribution exhibits a heavier right tail above $100,000, consistent with Canada's progressive marginal rate schedule compressing the post-tax distribution at the upper deciles. Source: Census 2021.

G9 · Census 2021

Poverty Prevalence by Province: LIM-AT vs. LICO-AT

LIM-AT systematically exceeds LICO-AT in all provinces (gap: ≈6–10 pp); Nunavut's LIM-AT rate (≈15%) is among the highest nationally despite LICO-AT's inapplicability to territories, underscoring the sensitivity of poverty measurement to relative versus absolute threshold choice. Source: Census 2021.

Poverty & Cross-Provincial Income Correlates

G15 · Census 2021

Median Income vs. Low-Income Prevalence (2021)

LIM-AT exhibits a strong negative correlation with median income (r = −0.89), mechanically consistent with its relative-threshold construction, while LICO-AT shows a near-zero positive correlation (r = 0.24); the divergence is most pronounced in the territories, where high median incomes produce low LIM-AT rates despite elevated cost-of-living-adjusted hardship. Source: Census 2021.

G18 · Census 2021

Visible Minority Population (%) × Median Income

The near-zero positive correlation (r = 0.11) between visible minority share and provincial median income is inconsistent with a wage-dilution hypothesis and reflects omitted confounders - principally industrial composition; NT and YT drive the positive trend through resource wage premiums, while high-minority ON and BC cluster near the sample income median. Source: Census 2021.

G19 · Census 2021

Postsecondary Attainment (%) × Median Income

Postsecondary attainment explains negligible cross-provincial income variation (R² = 0.048) in this 13-observation cross-section; NT and YT - above-median incomes despite moderate attainment - indicate that resource extraction wage premiums dominate any education-productivity channel at the provincial level of aggregation. Source: Census 2021.

Regression Models: Immigrant Composition & Outcomes

G13 · Census 2021

OLS: Median Income vs. Immigrant Region Shares (R² = 0.544)

Immigrant origin-region composition accounts for roughly half the cross-provincial variation in median income (R² = 0.544); notable residuals - ON and BC underpredicted, NT and YT well-fitted - suggest that immigrant region shares are a proxy for broader industrial structure rather than an independent causal channel. Source: Census 2021.

G14 · Census 2021

Logistic Model: High Poverty (HL χ² = 4.297, p = 0.231)

The logistic model achieves adequate calibration (HL χ² = 4.297, p = 0.231); Ontario's predicted probability of 0.81 at a 10% LIM-AT rate reflects covariate patterns - urban density and housing cost burden - that the model captures independently of the raw poverty rate. Source: Census 2021.

G12 · Census 2021

Employment Rate by Asia-share Tercile (ANOVA F = 5.945, p = 0.0199)

Mid-tercile provinces exhibit significantly higher median employment rates (≈62%) than low-tercile provinces (≈52%), with ANOVA rejecting equal means at the 5% level (F = 5.945, p = 0.020); the non-monotonic pattern is more consistent with omitted province-level economic conditions correlated with Asian immigrant settlement than a direct compositional effect. Source: Census 2021.

G11 · Census 2021

Immigrant Region × Education Heatmap (Cramér's V = 0.000)

African-origin immigrants exhibit a strong positive correlation (r = 0.72) with no-certificate attainment and a strong negative correlation (r = −0.70) with high school completion across provinces, while European immigrants show a pronounced positive association (r = 0.60) with postsecondary attainment; the near-zero Cramér's V reflects cancellation of opposing region-level effects rather than an absence of substantively large heterogeneities. Source: Census 2021.

Integration Analysis

Canada's economic-class focus produces an immigrant population with high formal credentials - over 60% of economic-class principal applicants hold a bachelor's degree or higher. Despite this, IMDB data document a persistent earnings gap at landing (60–70% of comparable Canadian-born wages in year one), with convergence to 85–95% by year 10. Cross-provincial regression analysis (G13, R² = 0.544) confirms that immigrant origin-region composition is a significant correlate of provincial median income, though the relationship is substantially mediated by industrial structure.

The divergence between LIM-AT and LICO-AT poverty metrics (G9, G15) highlights the methodological sensitivity of poverty comparisons across geographies with different cost structures and median incomes - a distinction of particular relevance for territorial jurisdictions.

Census 2021 Headline: Visible minority share (r = 0.11, G18) and postsecondary attainment (R² = 0.048, G19) both exhibit negligible unconditional correlations with provincial median income, pointing to industrial composition as the primary omitted variable in any province-level income regression.

Citizenship & Settlement

G1 · Census 2021

Citizenship Status by Province/Territory (2021 Census)

Canadian citizenship saturation exceeds 88% in all provinces; Nunavut (98.9%) and Newfoundland (97.8%) record the highest rates while BC (88.7%) and Ontario (90.0%) - the primary immigrant-destination provinces - show the lowest, consistent with their higher shares of recent arrivals still within the three-year naturalization waiting period. Source: Census 2021.

Household Structure

G17 · Census 2021

One-Person Households vs. Median Total Income (2021)

The near-zero correlation (r = 0.11) between one-person household prevalence and provincial median income indicates that single-person household share is not systematically associated with lower provincial incomes; high-income urban centres (ON, BC) and resource territories (YT, NT) simultaneously exhibit elevated one-person household rates for structurally distinct demographic and economic reasons. Source: Census 2021.

Provincial Analysis

Ontario continues to receive the largest share of permanent residents (approximately 45%), followed by British Columbia (15%) and Quebec (14%, with its own selection system). The PNP has significantly altered settlement dynamics: Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Alberta, and the Atlantic provinces have all seen substantial increases in immigrant inflows since PNP allocations were expanded in the mid-2000s.

The citizenship saturation data (G1) confirm that low-immigration provinces maintain citizenship rates above 94%, providing a baseline against which to assess the integration speed of recent cohorts. Cross-provincial household analysis (G17) suggests that household structure indicators carry limited predictive power for provincial income levels absent controls for industrial composition and resource endowment.

Demographic Profile

Immigrant origin, sex composition, income gaps, household structure, educational attainment, and occupational segregation. Source: Statistics Canada, Census 2021.

Immigration Origins

G2 · Census 2021

Immigrant Population by World Region of Birth (Canada, 2021)

Asia accounts for 51.5% (4.31M) of Canada's foreign-born population - exceeding the combined Europe and Americas shares - reflecting the cumulative effect of post-1990 policy shifts toward skills-based selection and the geographic origins of the principal applicant pool under Express Entry and the PNP. Source: Census 2021.

G4 · Census 2021

Visible Minority Categories (Canada, 2021)

South Asians (7.1%), Chinese (4.7%), and Black Canadians (4.3%) together comprise 16.1% of the total population; these shares reflect both historical immigration flows and differential fertility, with South Asian communities recording the fastest absolute growth in the 2016–2021 intercensal period. Source: Census 2021.

Sex, Income & Household Structure

G3 · Census 2021

Sex Composition and Median Income Gap (Canada, 2021)

Women's median income is 17–31% below men's across all five income concepts; the market income gap (31.1%) substantially exceeds the after-tax gap (17.6%), confirming that progressive taxation and transfers attenuate but do not eliminate the structural gender income differential — consistent with OECD-wide evidence on redistributive fiscal incidence. Source: Census 2021.

G5 · Census 2021

Household Type Distribution (Canada, 2021)

One-census-family households without additional persons dominate at 59.6%; the 2.9% multigenerational household share is disproportionately concentrated among immigrant communities from South and East Asia, where co-residence norms and settlement economics jointly suppress household dissolution rates. Source: Census 2021.

Education & Credentials

G6 · Census 2021

Educational Attainment (15+), by Sex (Canada, 2021)

Women have surpassed men at the bachelor's degree level (3.3M vs. 2.5M) and college/CEGEP certificates, consistent with the global reversal of the gender education gap among post-1980 cohorts; men retain a 2.2× advantage in apprenticeship/trades credentials, reflecting persistent gender segregation in vocational training pathways. Source: Census 2021.

Occupational Distribution & Gender Segregation

G10 · Census 2021

Broad Occupation Groups (NOC), by Sex (Canada, 2021)

Trades, transport, and equipment operators are male-dominated (men ≈3.0M vs. women ≈0.1M) while health and business/finance/administration skew heavily female; sales and service — the largest NOC group in absolute terms — exhibits near-parity (≈2.0M women, ≈1.9M men), reflecting the feminisation of high-volume service-sector employment. Source: Census 2021.

G20 · Census 2021

Gender Segregation by Occupation (NOC, Canada 2021)

Occupational gender segregation is most extreme in trades/transport (women's share: 8.9%) and health occupations (women's share: 80.1%); the implied dissimilarity index indicates that substantial cross-occupation reallocation would be required to achieve gender balance, with corresponding implications for wage convergence given the persistent wage penalty in female-dominated fields. Source: Census 2021.

Demographic Analysis

Canada's immigrant population is distinguished by high educational attainment and strong working-age concentration, with Asia constituting over half of the foreign-born population (G2). The sex-disaggregated income analysis (G3) documents a persistent gender income gap of 17–31% across income concepts, despite the narrowing of educational attainment gaps (G6) in which women now surpass men at the bachelor's degree level.

Occupational segregation (G10, G20) remains a structural feature of the Canadian labour market, with trades and transport retaining near-complete male dominance (women's share: 8.9%) and health occupations correspondingly female-dominated (80.1%). These patterns interact with credential distributions to produce persistent sectoral wage gaps that aggregate income measures partially obscure.

Census 2021 Headline: The market income gender gap (31.1%, G3) is nearly double the after-tax gap (17.6%), quantifying the redistributive contribution of Canada's tax-transfer system — and implying that policy changes to transfer programs would have differential incidence by sex.
