Executive Summary

The United States admits approximately 1.18 million legal permanent residents annually under a system that allocates the majority of visas through family-sponsored channels. Immigration has been a defining economic and demographic force: the foreign-born population has grown from 9.6 million in 1970 to over 50 million today, reshaping labor markets, fiscal accounts, and regional demographics across all 50 states.

This country report presents disaggregated data on admission flows, visa category distribution, source-country composition, labor market outcomes, geographic settlement patterns, and the measurable effects of major legislative changes. The ACS Demographics and Social & Integration tabs present data from the 2024 American Community Survey covering origin, naturalization, language, education, housing, and integration outcomes by entry cohort.

Key Takeaways

Approximately 65% of all permanent visas are allocated through family-sponsored preferences and immediate-relative admissions. This structural emphasis shapes the demographic and economic profile of new immigrants and differentiates the U.S. system fundamentally from Canada's economic-selection model.
The 7% per-country cap on employment-based green cards generates wait times exceeding 50 years for Indian nationals in some categories. This bottleneck affects talent retention, workforce planning, and international competitiveness in high-skilled labor markets.
While comprising 14.8% of the population, immigrants account for approximately 25% of U.S. patent filings and have founded or co-founded over 40% of Fortune 500 companies. Immigrant entrepreneurship rates exceed those of native-born Americans across most demographic cohorts.
Since 2000, the fastest growth in immigrant population share has occurred in states outside the traditional six gateways — particularly in the Southeast and Mountain West. This dispersion creates new demands on local infrastructure, education, and social services in communities with limited prior experience receiving immigrants.

Trend Analysis

Annual LPR admissions have ranged from approximately 370,000 in FY1970 to peaks exceeding 1.8 million during IRCA legalizations (FY1989–1991). Excluding the IRCA adjustment, the steady-state level has trended upward from roughly 500,000 in the 1970s to approximately 1.0–1.2 million since 2000, driven primarily by growth in immediate-relative admissions (which are not numerically capped) and modest increases in employment-based allocations.

The source-country composition has undergone a structural transformation. In 1970, six of the top ten source countries were in Europe. By 2024, the top five source countries are Mexico, India, China, the Philippines, and the Dominican Republic, collectively accounting for approximately 40% of all admissions.

Category Analysis

The U.S. permanent immigration system is divided into five principal channels: immediate relatives of citizens (numerically unlimited), family-sponsored preferences (~226,000), employment-based preferences (~140,000 base), diversity lottery (55,000), and refugees/asylees (variable). The immediate-relative category has grown substantially as a share of total admissions, reflecting both demographic momentum and policy choices that have not expanded preference-category caps commensurately with demand.

The employment-based system faces acute congestion. The 7% per-country ceiling means that nationals of high-demand countries — particularly India — face wait times that exceed the working lifetime of an applicant. This dynamic creates significant talent-retention risks for the United States relative to competitor economies, including Canada, that do not impose comparable per-country limits.

Earnings rise with tenure for both genders. Gender gap persists across cohorts: $6,637 (2010+) to $9,504 (Pre-2000), suggesting structural rather than assimilation-related drivers. Source: ACS 2024.

Median household income rises sharply from $76.6K (2010+) to $85.5K (2000–09), then plateaus at $85.3K (Pre-2000), suggesting income gains concentrate in the first decade of settlement. Source: ACS 2024.

Economic Analysis

Immigrants constitute approximately 17% of the U.S. civilian labor force. ACS 2024 data show labor force participation peaks among the 2000–09 cohort (77.2%), reflecting the prime working-age concentration of this group. Recent arrivals (2010+) show higher unemployment (6.0%) and lower household incomes ($76.6K median), consistent with standard labor market assimilation patterns.

Poverty rates decline substantially with tenure: the 2010+ cohort shows 18.1% poverty vs. 10.8% for Pre-2000 arrivals. Family structure is a strong moderating factor — female-headed households (no spouse) face rates of 33.6% (2010+) compared to 11.2% for married-couple families in the same cohort. Occupation distributions are broadly similar across cohorts, with management/professional roles representing approximately 38–39% of employed immigrants regardless of tenure.

ACS 2024 Headline: The gender earnings gap among full-time workers ranges from $6,637 (2010+ cohort) to $9,504 (Pre-2000), suggesting the gap widens rather than narrows over immigrant tenure — a pattern warranting further decomposition by occupation and industry.

Settlement Analysis

ACS 2024 mobility data show that the South received the largest absolute volume of international movers (1.117 million), more than the Northeast (451K), West (609K), and Midwest (375K) combined. This concentration reflects the continued growth of Sun Belt metropolitan areas as primary immigrant destinations — a structural shift from the traditional Northeast/West gateway dominance of earlier decades.

At the regional level, the South (0.85%) and Northeast (0.79%) show the highest international mover share as a proportion of regional population, compared to 0.77% in the West and 0.54% in the Midwest. The Midwest's lower rate is consistent with its slower overall population growth and fewer traditional immigrant gateway cities. Six traditional gateway states — California, New York, Texas, Florida, New Jersey, and Illinois — still account for roughly 65% of the cumulative foreign-born stock.

Legislative Impact Assessment

Each major piece of immigration legislation has produced measurable, and often lagged, effects on admission volumes, category distributions, and enforcement metrics. The Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965 triggered a structural shift in source-country composition that unfolded over two decades. IRCA produced a temporary spike in admissions through legalization but did not achieve its stated goal of reducing unauthorized immigration. IIRIRA introduced expedited removal and 3/10-year bars that continue to shape unauthorized-to-authorized transition dynamics.

This section will present regression-discontinuity and event-study estimates quantifying the causal impact of major legislative changes on admission flows, processing times, and enforcement outcomes.

ACS 2024 Demographics

Origin composition, nativity, age structure, and ancestry of the U.S. foreign-born population by entry cohort. Source: ACS 1-Year Estimates 2024.

Origin & Composition

G03 · ACS 2024

Total Foreign-Born Origin Composition

Latin America accounts for over half (51.4%) of the foreign-born population, followed by Asia (30.8%) and Europe (9.7%). Africa's share (5.9%) has grown rapidly in recent cohorts. Source: ACS 2024.

G01 · ACS 2024

Origin Region by Entry Cohort

Africa's share rises from 3.5% (Pre-2000) to 8.3% (2010+), while Europe's share declines from 12.4% to 7.5% across cohorts — reflecting shifting global migration patterns. Source: ACS 2024.

Nativity & Age Structure

G42 · ACS DP02 2024

Nativity Status of U.S. Population

14.8% of the U.S. population (approximately 50.2 million people) is foreign-born — the highest absolute level in the nation's history. Source: ACS DP02 2024.

G43 · ACS DP02 2024

Foreign-Born Population by Period of Entry

37.7% of the foreign-born population entered in 2010 or later, and 41.2% arrived before 2000 — reflecting both recent inflows and the long-established immigrant stock. Source: ACS DP02 2024.

G37 · ACS 2024

Age Distribution by Entry Cohort

2010+ arrivals are concentrated in the 18–44 bracket (61.1%); Pre-2000 arrivals have aged into the 45–64 (49.2%) and 65+ (35.6%) brackets, reflecting natural cohort aging. Source: ACS 2024.

G38 · ACS 2024

Median Age of Foreign-Born by Entry Cohort

Median age rises from 33.6 years (2010+) to 59.6 years (Pre-2000). The 26-year gap between cohorts is driven by natural aging of long-settled immigrants rather than differences in entry age. Source: ACS 2024.

Ancestry

G44 · ACS DP02 2024

Top Ancestry Groups, All U.S. Residents

German (11.8%), Irish (9.5%), and English (9.1%) remain the three largest self-reported ancestry groups, reflecting the deep European heritage of the native-born population alongside growing sub-Saharan African ancestry. Source: ACS DP02 2024.

Demographics Analysis

The ACS 2024 documents a foreign-born population of approximately 50.2 million — 14.8% of the total U.S. population. Latin America accounts for 51.4% of this population (weighted heavily by Mexico), with Asia (30.8%) now approaching half of Latin America's share. Critically, Africa's share has nearly tripled across entry cohorts from 3.5% (Pre-2000) to 8.3% (2010+), signaling a fundamental diversification of immigrant origins.

Age structure follows a predictable cohort-aging pattern: the 2010+ group has a median age of 33.6 years and is concentrated in the working-age 18–44 bracket, while the Pre-2000 cohort has a median age of 59.6 years and is increasingly concentrated in the 45–64 and 65+ brackets. This demographic gradient has important implications for labor supply, naturalization propensity, and government transfer program utilization.

ACS 2024 Headline: Sub-Saharan African ancestry now appears in the top 10 ancestry groups at 1.4%, and Africa's origin share among recent arrivals (8.3%) is more than twice its share among Pre-2000 immigrants (3.5%).

Social Profile & Integration

Naturalization, language proficiency, educational attainment, homeownership, household structure, and integration metrics by entry cohort. Source: ACS 1-Year Estimates 2024.

Naturalization

G05 · ACS 2024

Naturalization Rate by Entry Cohort

Only 22.6% of 2010+ arrivals are naturalized, compared to 75.9% of Pre-2000 immigrants — a strong assimilation gradient that reflects both eligibility requirements and the time required for integration. Source: ACS 2024.

G06 · ACS 2024

Citizenship Status by Entry Cohort

77.4% of the 2010+ cohort are non-citizens vs. only 24.1% of Pre-2000 arrivals. The inversion from non-citizen majority to citizen majority occurs between the 2010+ and 2000–09 cohorts. Source: ACS 2024.

Language Proficiency

G10 · ACS DP02 2024

Language Spoken at Home, All U.S. Residents

77.0% of U.S. residents speak only English at home. Spanish (13.9%) is the dominant non-English language, followed by Other Indo-European (4.1%) and Asian/Pacific Islander languages (3.7%). Source: ACS DP02 2024.

G09 · ACS DP02 2024

Limited English Proficiency Rate by Language Group

Asian/PI speakers have the highest LEP rate (43.1%), slightly above Spanish speakers (41.1%). Indo-European speakers show a lower rate (31.5%), reflecting the European-origin English-cognate advantage. Source: ACS DP02 2024.

Education & Housing

G13 · ACS 2024

Educational Attainment by Entry Cohort (Population 25+)

2010+ arrivals have the highest share with less than high school (19.4%) but also the highest BA+ attainment (44.6%), indicating a bimodal distribution. Pre-2000 immigrants show the most concentrated less-than-HS group (27.0%). Source: ACS 2024.

G15 · ACS 2024

Bachelor's Degree or Higher by Entry Cohort

Recent arrivals have the highest educational credentials (44.6% BA+) — consistent with increased selectivity in employment-based admissions and graduate student-to-worker transitions. Source: ACS 2024.

G28 · ACS 2024

Homeownership Rate by Entry Cohort

Homeownership rises from 32.0% (2010+) to 69.3% (Pre-2000), tracking closely with naturalization rates and income trajectories. The 37-point gap between cohorts signals sustained economic integration over time. Source: ACS 2024.

Household Structure & Integration Profile

G32 · ACS 2024

Marital Status Distribution by Entry Cohort

Married rates rise with tenure (53.8% → 63.9%). "Never married" declines sharply from 37.0% (2010+) to 12.7% (Pre-2000), partially reflecting age distribution differences between cohorts. Source: ACS 2024.

G53 · ACS 2024

Integration Profile Radar by Entry Cohort

Pre-2000 immigrants score highest on structural integration (naturalization, homeownership, marriage, low poverty) while 2010+ arrivals lead on educational credentials (BA+ 44.6%). Source: ACS 2024.

Integration Analysis

ACS 2024 data paint a consistent picture of immigrant integration as a decades-long trajectory. Naturalization (22.6% → 75.9%), homeownership (32.0% → 69.3%), marriage rates (53.8% → 63.9%), and income all improve substantially across entry cohorts. Poverty rates halve from 18.1% (2010+) to 10.8% (Pre-2000).

Language remains the most persistent integration barrier. LEP rates are high for recent arrivals and do not fully close even in long-settled cohorts: 43.0% of Pre-2000 non-English speakers still speak English less than "very well." Asian/Pacific Islander language speakers face the highest LEP rates (43.1%), followed by Spanish speakers (41.1%).

Education shows a bimodal pattern among recent arrivals: the 2010+ cohort has both the highest BA+ share (44.6%) and the highest less-than-high-school share (19.4%), reflecting the dual nature of the U.S. immigration system which selects simultaneously for high-skill workers and for family-sponsored and humanitarian arrivals.

ACS 2024 Headline: Naturalization rate more than triples from 2010+ (22.6%) to Pre-2000 cohorts (75.9%); homeownership follows the same trajectory (+37 points), confirming that structural integration — civic and economic — is a long-horizon process requiring at least 10–15 years to materialize fully.
