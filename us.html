Executive Summary

The United States admits approximately 1.18 million legal permanent residents annually under a system that allocates the majority of visas through family-sponsored channels. Immigration has been a defining economic and demographic force: the foreign-born population has grown from 9.6 million in 1970 to over 45 million today, reshaping labor markets, fiscal accounts, and regional demographics across all 50 states.

This country report presents disaggregated data on admission flows, visa category distribution, source-country composition, labor market outcomes, geographic settlement patterns, and the measurable effects of major legislative changes. Navigate through the indicator tabs above to explore each dimension.

Key Takeaways

Approximately 65% of all permanent visas are allocated through family-sponsored preferences and immediate-relative admissions. This structural emphasis shapes the demographic and economic profile of new immigrants and differentiates the U.S. system fundamentally from Canada's economic-selection model.
The 7% per-country cap on employment-based green cards generates wait times exceeding 50 years for Indian nationals in some categories. This bottleneck affects talent retention, workforce planning, and international competitiveness in high-skilled labor markets.
While comprising 14.3% of the population, immigrants account for approximately 25% of U.S. patent filings and have founded or co-founded over 40% of Fortune 500 companies. Immigrant entrepreneurship rates exceed those of native-born Americans across most demographic cohorts.
Since 2000, the fastest growth in immigrant population share has occurred in states outside the traditional six gateways — particularly in the Southeast and Mountain West. This dispersion creates new demands on local infrastructure, education, and social services in communities with limited prior experience receiving immigrants.

Trend Analysis

Annual LPR admissions have ranged from approximately 370,000 in FY1970 to peaks exceeding 1.8 million during IRCA legalizations (FY1989–1991). Excluding the IRCA adjustment, the steady-state level has trended upward from roughly 500,000 in the 1970s to approximately 1.0–1.2 million since 2000, driven primarily by growth in immediate-relative admissions (which are not numerically capped) and modest increases in employment-based allocations.

The source-country composition has undergone a structural transformation. In 1970, six of the top ten source countries were in Europe. By 2024, the top five source countries are Mexico, India, China, the Philippines, and the Dominican Republic, collectively accounting for approximately 40% of all admissions.

Category Analysis

The U.S. permanent immigration system is divided into five principal channels: immediate relatives of citizens (numerically unlimited), family-sponsored preferences (~226,000), employment-based preferences (~140,000 base), diversity lottery (55,000), and refugees/asylees (variable). The immediate-relative category has grown substantially as a share of total admissions, reflecting both demographic momentum and policy choices that have not expanded preference-category caps commensurately with demand.

The employment-based system faces acute congestion. The 7% per-country ceiling means that nationals of high-demand countries — particularly India — face wait times that exceed the working lifetime of an applicant. This dynamic creates significant talent-retention risks for the United States relative to competitor economies, including Canada, that do not impose comparable per-country limits.

Economic Analysis

Immigrants constitute approximately 17% of the U.S. civilian labor force. They are concentrated in sectors with persistent labor shortages: agriculture (estimated 73% of crop workers are foreign-born), construction (30%), accommodation and food services (28%), and healthcare support (25%). At the high-skill end, immigrants represent 29% of STEM workers and account for roughly 25% of U.S. patent filings.

The aggregate fiscal impact of immigration is net-positive at the federal level and varies at the state/local level depending on immigrant educational attainment, household size, and utilization of means-tested programs. The National Academies (2017) found that immigration generates a net positive fiscal contribution of $279,000 in present-value terms over a 75-year horizon when second-generation effects are included.

Settlement Analysis

Six traditional gateway states — California, New York, Texas, Florida, New Jersey, and Illinois — account for roughly 65% of the foreign-born population. However, the fastest growth rates since 2000 have occurred in new-destination states: North Carolina, Georgia, Tennessee, Nevada, and Arkansas have seen their immigrant populations more than double. This geographic dispersion creates varied demands on education, healthcare, and language-access services at the local level.

Legislative Impact Assessment

Each major piece of immigration legislation has produced measurable, and often lagged, effects on admission volumes, category distributions, and enforcement metrics. The Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965 triggered a structural shift in source-country composition that unfolded over two decades. IRCA produced a temporary spike in admissions through legalization but did not achieve its stated goal of reducing unauthorized immigration. IIRIRA introduced expedited removal and 3/10-year bars that continue to shape unauthorized-to-authorized transition dynamics.
